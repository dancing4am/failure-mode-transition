# Cover Letter — Nature Communications

---

Dear Editors,

I am pleased to submit **"Why Fixed Protections Fail Under Rising Coordination: A Structural Failure-Mode Transition in Coupled Systems"** for consideration as a research article in *Nature Communications*.

**What we found.** Modern governance relies on passive stabilizers — fixed-rule mechanisms like portfolio diversification, constitutional checks, and AI safety filters that buffer shocks at fixed strength. We show that these mechanisms are structurally insufficient at high coupling. In mean-field coordination models, a fixed protective field loses effectiveness as the square of the coupling, with an irreducible residual at high coupling that an active (scaling) stabilizer eliminates entirely. The framework yields a structurally unique prediction — a **failure-mode transition**: as coupling rises, the *type* of failure shifts from fragmentation to rigidity. We confirm this in 22 years of S&P 500 sector data — the model's predicted type-composition curve matches the empirical curve with near-perfect rank correlation — and replicate the pattern in cryptocurrency markets, where supercritical coupling is the typical regime.

**Why it matters.** The finding reframes governance design: the question is not whether protections are strong enough but whether they are the right *kind*. Current AI governance — capability evaluations, content filters, alignment fine-tuning — falls almost entirely on the passive side. Direct measurement of frontier-model output coupling places AI above the model's critical threshold, and universal jailbreaks — adversarial strings that bypass multiple models' independent safety training simultaneously — instantiate exactly the passive-stabilizer failure the framework predicts. The paper does not merely describe the failure mode; it derives the structural class to which any effective intervention must belong: mechanisms whose response scales with the measured threat.

**Why *Nature Communications*.** This work synthesizes statistical physics (Curie–Weiss mean-field theory), financial economics (asymmetric dependence, diversification failure), AI safety (adversarial transfer), and comparative politics (institutional erosion under polarization) into a single framework. *Nature Communications*' interdisciplinary mission and recent record in complex-systems research applied to social dynamics — including Iacopini et al. (2024), Horsevad et al. (2022), and Ferraz de Arruda et al. (2023) — make it the natural venue for a contribution that sits across disciplines no single-field journal can adequately assess.
