# arXiv submission metadata

## Title

Why Fixed Protections Fail Under Rising Coordination: A Structural Failure-Mode Transition in Coupled Systems

## Author

Chowon Jung

## Abstract

Rising coordination is tightening the coupling between once-independent agents. Governance relies on passive stabilizers (diversification, constitutional checks, automatic income supports) that buffer shocks at fixed strength. We identify a structural prediction unique to coordination dynamics: as coupling rises, the *type* of failure transitions from fragmentation to rigidity. This is confirmed in 22 years of S&P 500 data — drawdown-period correlation rises 0.50 to 0.88 across coupling deciles, rigidity share 0% to 100%, with empirical-model rank correlation rho = +0.92. The underlying scaling law: a fixed field h opposing coupling J loses effectiveness as h/J^2 with an irreducible residual. The pattern survives across alternative model classes (Curie-Weiss, voter, Kuramoto, cubic), topologies, and a second domain (cryptocurrency, 64% supercritical vs ~25% equities). An active stabilizer eliminates the residual. A heuristic AI output-similarity proxy (rho ≈ 0.80, J_eff ≈ 4.0; 14 models x 805 prompts) locates frontier AI above the critical threshold (J_c = 2), in the regime where passive effectiveness decays; the mapping is heuristic, not calibrated. Overcoming the asymmetry requires active stabilizers.
